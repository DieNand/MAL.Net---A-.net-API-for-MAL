'Charlotte' is the latest work of Maeda Jun, the director of 'Angel Beats!' and composer of 'Clannad', 'Air' and 'Little Busters!'. All of these are good series, but can 'Charlotte' keep up with this pattern?

|Story|
The pacing is not the best, instead 'Charlotte' starts slow, but the main part of the story will probably be compressed into the second half. What makes it interesting are the relationships within the main cast, especially Tomori and Yuu.

|Art|
What certainly is confusing is the collision between foreground and background. While latter is incredibly detailed, the characters lack something, though it can't be their eyes, those are shown with reflections and other beautiful effects.
Colour-wise the whole pallete comes to use, lens flares contribute their share to the atmosphere.

|Sound|
You may remember the third episode of 'Angel Beats!', when Iwasawa sung out her heart and soul. 'Charlotte' is also focused on music, but the singing lacks the passion we experienced in 'Angel Beats!'.
The OST and voice acting on the other side makes up fot the small amount of singing, it's mostly made out of quiet electronic music. That is, contrary to my expectations, not debilitating, but strengthens the mood and gives 'Charlotte' a modern touch. The OP is stunning together with the visuals and the ED isn't bad either, though it doesn't stay in your head like other Endings (Brave, Dango Daikazoku)

|Character|
While Yusa(-rin) and Joujirou are upbeat, comedic characters, Tomori and Yuu are opposed to them in terms of triggering viewer reaction. They are the reason for the majority of melancolic themes and scenes in 'Charlotte'.
Character development is pretty strong, but it could be more spread on the whole cast.

|Enjoyment|
I tend to see 'Charlotte' as my favourite anime of the season, mainly because of its alternation between humor and melancholy.
Maeda Jun is not known for failing at scenarios or soundtrack or anything else, the remainning five episodes are probably going to raise the bar.

|Recommendation|
If you like a combination of slice-of -life and mystery, then this might be for you. But if you have seen Juns previous works, then 'Charlotte' will seem like the younger brother of these series to you.

//A Midseason Review - To Be Updated//
//Spoilers for most recent episodes//

Charlotte, unfortunately, holds the title of "Most Disappointing Anime" of the season (so far) for me, for several reasons, all of them related to the core issue that Jun Maeda is trying to reuse his old formulas by taking bits and pieces of "Hey, this made people cry!" and shoving them all into one show - and that just flat doesn't work.

Let's start with the good. First and foremost, Charlotte holds OP of the Season, hands down - Bravely You is catchy and memorable and, in a way, carries a tone of forward-moving uncertainty and wonder that the show kinda has as a theme (see below). Coupled with a blend of Key and P.A. Works' eyegasmic animation and you can also give "visuals nearly on par with Ufotable" to the plus marks.

The show *starts* on a very strong note, with a very intriguing premise and a refreshing cast of characters. Yuu was like a reimagined Lelouch, Nao had a razor sharp wit, and Joujirou had a very solid straight man streak going (which contrasted well against his, the most ridiculous, of powers). Even Ayumi managed to get a good combo of archetypical imouto-ness with some deeper character backing it up. If there's one thing Jun Maeda is good at, it's initial character design and premises.

However, one thing that Jun Maeda (and by extension, Key Visual as a studio) fails miserably at is... well, actually telling a story. It happened with Clannad, it happened with Angel Beats, and it happened with Charlotte - so much time is spent on poorly paced "character-building" episodes that there's no time for the development of an engaging plot. Even now, after seven (!) episodes of a one-cour show, **Charlotte does not have a coherent plot.** It keeps veering back and forth between slice-of-life and drama without giving focus to either element in sufficient quantities to develop them. How would one even summarize the show? Is it about some kids dealing with weird powers in the face of conspiracy? No, those plot elements are just taken as givens and not explored. Is it about overcoming emotional traumas in your past as you grow up? Maybe, but there isn't much on that front, either!

This is made more noticeable by how the characterization absolutely FLOPS after episode three. Yuu's hilarious pseudo-villainy is dropped faster than panties in Highschool DxD and his personality varies from episode to episode - I'd almost call him a VN protagonist, he's so passive, and maybe that's Key's bad habits showing. What's a real shame though is that Nao's wit devolves into "All according to plan" every single episode, Yusa exists as an unfulfilling comedy element, and Joujirou gets turned into the butt of a flat-out unfunny "such a turn-off!" joke with nothing of his original personality to redeem himself by.

The stagnation of the character development is what really bogged the show down from episodes 2 to 6, since it played out like an episodic slice-of-life but with nothing to show for it - anything we learned about our characters was either cliche or outright ignored in later episodes - take, for instance, how Yuu is consistently surprised that Joujirou is repeatedly subjected to horrific injury every episode.

We spend 6 episodes "building character," but the problem is the more time you spend looking at them the flatter they get, which I think goes without saying is the opposite of what should be happening.

On top of that, throw on the technical sloppiness. Camerawork, scripting, scene cut timings - there are sloppy mistakes *everywhere.* For instance, the ending of episode 6 features a zoom-cut that's supposed to match the tension of the scene: except it *breaks* the rule of a zoom cut in that it lingers on Yuu's face and pans around a bit. Sure, the show is *animated* well, but that doesn't make up for poor storyboarding and timings! Another example is the abrupt ending of *every* episode, particularly 1-5; those episodes end, without fail, with a pseudo-philosophical conversation between Yuu and Ayumi before cutting straight to credits. The episodes end leaving you wondering "Wait, that's it?" and not in the anticipatory sense.

But it wouldn't be Key anime without Key Tears™. And good golly, did this swing around the quality scale. Episode 7 has what is possibly the most blatant waste of emotional capital I've seen since Aldnoah Zero S2. "Oh, you're awake! Your sister's dead, sorry. [Roll opening credits]." While the intent might've been to deliver an abrupt punch to the gut, the effect was instead emotional backlash with the entire scene feeling forced and *intentionally* dramatic - it's like how you can't really feel sad when Barbara cheats on Stan in afternoon soap operas; the emotional tension is so visibly manufactured you can almost see the "CRY NOW" prompt at the top of the screen.

However, the rest of episode 7 does manage to fulfill Maeda's trademark specialty of generating absolute despair. Yuu's descent into depression and self-destruction is painful not because of the hardships he's enduring but because of the knowledge that he is *self-inflicting* those hardships. While the previous episodes had their faults, the audience *has* built rapport with Yuu, and seeing him destroy himself is a painful thing to watch, even if subconsciously it's still obvious that the show is using some narrative tricks to do it. And, y'know what? Nao showing up at the end was a legitimately surprising and heartwarming moment.

...but then, whiplash again, because the episode ends with the implication that all of this has been resolved. Yuu's back on the straight and narrow (though whether he stays there IS a matter of the next five episodes) and Key Magic™ has brought our protagonist from the brink of despair - except the whole "Well, I had my depressive, self-destructive suicide binge, that wraps up that emotional subplot" tone the end of the episode conveys near single-handedly undoes all the emotional investment built up.

Charlotte is, like a lot of Key/PA Works anime, a show that had *fantastic* promise and every reason to be a masterpiece. However, whether it's a result of sloppiness, poor planning, or outright hubris it falls short in so many areas that it's maddening. It's a show where I *want* to like it and I *want* it to be fantastic, but it slowly bleeds out from the death of a thousand cuts. Heck, the show's plot up to this point is a fairly accurate metaphor for its own quality and the audience's reaction - bright interest, boredom, and then soul-crushing horror at the tragedy taking place.

Charlotte has 5 episodes left - maybe it can be saved by a little Key Magic™ in real life, but I just don't see what else could be up their sleeve to go off of from here. read more

NOTE: This review is subject to change as the episode count increases. This is my opinion as of episode 9.

Charlotte is probably the most hyped show this season for a good reason. Jun Maeda is the man behind Clannad and Kanon 2006 and he also collaborated with PA Works in the hit Angel Beats! last 2010. The two paired again this time yet it is not exactly as great like the last one.

Story: 5/10

The first episode of Charlotte was probably the one I liked the most from a Key anime. It was as if it peeked some from Kyoto Animation's joke book and implemented it PA Works style. The next two episodes aired and Charlotte was still coherent as we were introduced to other main characters. The jokes were still funny that time and Maeda did a commendable job for introducing us the world the characters have and their circumstances in a span of three episodes.

Then episode four came then it became the worst Charlotte episode which showed the dullest baseball match from Key and aired twenty minutes of nothingness. It has been erratic since then. Episode five is a bit better but could've been good if it focused on the interaction between the two main leads and showed some of their circumstances, especially Yuu's. In addition, it suffered from horrible fast pacing given that its preceding episode is a filler.

Given that we already know the main story since episode 9 is now released, my opinion is that these two episodes should've never been aired after all because it did not add anything in the show. One may argue that these are shown to focus with the character development of the four leads but Takajo and Yusa have only been used for comedic purposes so far and they did not contribute anything with Charlotte's legitimately good episodes (8 and 9).

Episode six and seven were better by a lot, BUT Maeda has shown his glaring weaknesses in the past with these two. Introducing a new character in the former (Ayumi's yandere schoolmate) to move the plot midcour is a good idea in paper but Charlotte's execution was not good enough to warrant her importance. Ayumi is not also given enough background aside from a pizza sauce loving imouto to garner sadness and sympathy from the audience. Magically, the yandere disappeared in the next episode. The latter showed how good Maeda is in portraying depression (Yuu's devolving into some kind of a bad person) but its effect was reduced because it has to be hooked with good past episodes for some sense of belief. Also, the problem was resolved all too quick and shows its ugly problem of execution.

Things get better with episode eight but it is still plagued by some questionable execution with its first half. The humor feels off and I sincerely believe that Takajo was reduced to a comic relief that is a hit or miss since episode three. However, this episode benefited from some organic drama that somehow forged their (Nao, her brother and him) connection further.

What made me really frustrated with it is the sheer greatness of episode 9. That episode alone is enough to change my perception with this show from meh to something special. It's perfectly executed, the plot is evenly paced and it gave answers to the fundamental questions the viewers have in this show.

For those who find this review too long, I say that this anime starts to get better starting episode six and finally became good at episode 9. The first five episodes including the last half of chapter six are uneven with episode 4 being the worst Key episode in my opinion. However, the last four episodes are not enough to cover the fillers it had in the past. Should Charlotte continue the goodness it presented episode seven onwards, this may be one of the better shows this season.

Art: 8/10

While not at the level of Kyoto Animations when it comes in adapting Key works, this is one of the best looking animes this season. The character designs are pretty good to the extent that I can differentiate them from other PA Works show. The hues used were also masterfully used in this show. Light colors are used during comedic scenes but when something ominous is about to happen, the shades change to very dark colors. Artwork is also consistent with only minor hiccups (some in episode 3 and 4) but not noticeable enough to distract the viewers.

The animation is also good and is mostly well timed with the jokes and crucial events. The background drawings also have good detail which can be said as one of their best.

Sound: 8/10

The opening song of Charlotte is probably my most favorite this season. As regards to background music, it has been shown in the past that Key animes are strong musically and this one is not an exception.

Character: 3/10

This is the worst aspect of the show because aside from Yuu, all other characters (even Nao and Ayumi) are sorely underdeveloped so far. The characters are mostly plot device and serve for comedic purposes. Even with its back-to-back good episodes, I suppose that its problem with giving proper role and development with its characters is its biggest flaw.

Nao is mostly unlikable even with her tragic backstory but at least she is aware of it. Ayumi was not given much development aside from a pizza loving imouto and a Yusarin fan so not everyone will feel Maeda wants to portray when something major happened to her.

Yusarin's could have been an interesting character given her circumstances but she was set aside to become the mascot girl of the Student Council. Worse is Takajo, who devolved from a respectable sidekick of Nao in episode 1 to a comic relief that is mostly not funny. Episode 9 only made it worse since these two may not have anything that contributed to the show when seen as a big picture.

In addition, the Zhiend vocalist only appeared midway through episode eight and practically stole the show in that moment which is good to introduce a character only when you are attached with the leads. Unfortunately, this is not Charlotte's case. Ayumi's yandere classmate is the worst case, since her act moved Charlotte in another direction but just disappeared the next episode. She has been missing since then.


Enjoyment: 4/10

I am a fan of Key and Clannad After Story stands as one of my most favorite animes of all time. However, I really do not know if Maeda has lost his potency to write powerful scenes because Charlotte has more misses than hits when it comes to drama and the comedy also lost its potency episode four onwards. It recovered on episode eight but a normal viewer will not wait for that long to see its real beauty. read more
